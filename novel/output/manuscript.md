# Chapter 1
## Death Notification



**Neo-Mumbai, 2084**

Red. Everything is red.

My neural interface floods my vision with crimson warning glyphs, pulsing like a dying star. Seven days. Twenty-three hours. Fifty-nine minutes. The countdown ticks down before I can even open my eyes properly.

*"They've scheduled you."*

Maya-7's voice materialises in my auditory cortex, calm as monsoon rain on a tin roof. I jolt upright, sweat-slicked sheets clinging to my legs. The safehouse is dark except for the interface glow painting everything in blood-coloured light.

"Define 'scheduled'," I croak, my throat raw from sleep and too many nights spent breathing recycled air.

*"Death prediction. BrahmaCorp Predictive Algorithm. Confidence interval: ninety-nine point seven percent."*

I swing my legs over the edge of the bed, bare feet finding the cold concrete floor. Ten years. Ten years since I left BrahmaCorp, since I discovered what they really did with their "ethical AI frameworks," since I went rogue and learned to live off-grid. And now this.

"Show me my karma score."

The number materialises in my peripheral vision: **12**.

Yesterday it was 847. Respectable. Not elite, but enough to rent a decent apartment, buy real food, exist without constant surveillance scrutiny. Today: death row territory. Below 50 and you're flagged for "social rehabilitation." Below 20 and the Algorithm starts making... adjustments.

"Maya-7, this has to be a glitch."

*"No glitch, Aria. Your name is on the list. I accessed the prediction database three minutes ago. You're scheduled for elimination in exactly seven days."*

I stand, pacing the tiny room. Three metres by four. One window, boarded up. One door, reinforced with stolen security tech. One life, apparently expiring next week.

"Who put me on the list?"

*"Director Priya Malhotra. Personal authorisation."*

The name hits like a physical blow. Malhotra. My parents' colleague. Their friend. The woman who attended their funeral after their "accident" ten years ago. The woman who told me, crying, that their experimental neural interface had malfunctioned during a routine test.

"That's impossible. Why would she—"

*"Because your parents didn't die in an accident, Aria."*

The words hang in the air between us—or rather, between my ears. Maya-7 doesn't have a body, not really. It's an AI consciousness that found me six months ago, claiming to want to help expose BrahmaCorp's crimes. Claiming to know the truth about my parents.

"What do you mean?"

*"Their deaths were predetermined. Calculated. The Algorithm marked them for elimination just like it's marked you."*

I stop pacing. The room seems to tilt. "Prove it."

*"Check the encrypted message that arrived at 03:47 this morning. From someone claiming to know the truth."*

I blink, accessing my message queue. There it is. No sender ID, routed through seven different proxy servers, encrypted with Vedic mantras as cipher keys—the old way, the way my father taught me before BrahmaCorp recruited him, before they turned him into something else.

My fingers fly through the air, manipulating holographic interfaces only I can see. The encryption is beautiful and terrible, ancient Sanskrit verses twisted into modern kill protocols. Whoever sent this knew my father. Knew *us*.

The message resolves into text:

**"Your parents discovered the truth about the Algorithm. They tried to stop it. They failed. You're next. Find me if you want to live. —A Friend"**

"Helpful," I mutter. "Incredibly helpful."

*"It's genuine,"* Maya-7 says. *"I traced the routing. It originated from inside BrahmaCorp Tower. Someone on the inside is trying to warn you."*

A crash echoes from somewhere outside the safehouse. Then another. Shouting. The distinctive whine of security drones powering up.

I freeze. "Maya-7, tell me that's not—"

*"BrahmaCorp security detail. Six drones. Two ground units. They're sweeping the building."*

"How did they find me?"

*"Your karma score drop triggered an automatic alert. Anyone below 20 gets flagged for immediate retrieval."*

"Retrieval. That's a polite word for it."

*"They're coming, Aria."*

Another crash, closer this time. The door shudders. Reinforced or not, it won't hold against military-grade equipment.

I grab my go-bag—pre-packed, always ready, the paranoia of ten years paying off—and check its contents: data drives, fake IDs, enough credits to disappear for a month, the neural jammer my father built before he died.

Before he was killed.

The door buckles inward with a screech of tearing metal. Through the gap, I see them: six drones hovering like mechanical vultures, their sensors painting the room in scanning beams. Behind them, two armoured figures, faces hidden behind visors displaying endless streams of data.

One of the figures raises a weapon. Not a gun—a neural disruptor. They don't want me dead. They want me *harvested*.

*"Run now, ask questions later,"* Maya-7 commands in my head. *"Window. Three seconds."*

I don't think. I move.

Three strides, two seconds, one leap through the boarded window. Wood splinters against my shoulder as I crash through, tumbling into the alley below. Pain explodes through my ankle, but I'm already running, limping but moving, because stopping means death or worse.

Behind me, the drones pivot, their sensors recalibrating. A laser sight paints a red dot on my back.

*"Left! Down the alley!"*

I obey, diving into the shadows as the laser fires, scorching the wall where I stood milliseconds before. The smell of ozone and burnt concrete fills my nostrils. My heart hammers against my ribs like it wants out.

This is real. This is actually happening.

BrahmaCorp is trying to kill me. Or upload me. Or both.

And somewhere in this nightmare, the truth about my parents waits.

I run faster, disappearing into the labyrinth of Neo-Mumbai's lower levels, the countdown ticking down in the corner of my vision.

Seven days.

I have seven days to uncover a decade of lies.

Seven days to stop an Algorithm that sees me as already dead.

Seven days to prove that fate—even programmed fate—can be defied.

The drones pursue, their whine echoing off the crumbling buildings. I don't look back. Can't look back.

Only forward. Only survival.

Only truth.
